Women's 'voluntary' and uniquely underpaid care work undergirds our economy and underwrites the guarantee of primary healthcare in India, as adopted by the Indian government via the Alma-Ata Declaration. In Kerala, a state with a long history of women's movements and worker mobilising, ASHA workers — the true functionaries of this healthcare system — organised a 266-day strike to demand recognition and compensation for their work.

In all the years I've spent as a journalist, I can think of stories of ASHAs (Accredited Social Health Activist) from almost every state I've worked in.

In the thick of conflict-reporting in Manipur, I met an ASHA worker who, for more than a year, spent her own money to ensure that immunisation happened in a tribal district that was completely cut off from regular medical supplies.

In the midst of an increase in Japanese Encephalitis deaths in the Malkangiri district of Chhattisgarh, I met an ASHA worker who spoke the truth about why infants were dropping dead in the district. A tribal woman herself, she spoke at length about the deep-rooted apathy against tribals in Orissa and the corruption within the Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS).

## During the Covid-19 pandemic, every district I visited in Tamil Nadu had ASHA workers out on the street every day

These are stories that would fit right into the trope of superhero films, where a man emerges from nowhere and saves a world hurtling towards an unimaginable calamity. But here, the 'heroes' are women from across the country. There is no thrilling entry; these women are already present, and probably from your neighbourhood. Our familiarity with them — or patriarchy — is one among the many reasons they will never be called 'heroes' and receive only the double burden of being women workers.

## How the system was designed

In 1978, the Alma-Ata Declaration led by WHO-UNICEF concluded that focussing on primary healthcare through community health workers is critical to achieving an acceptable level of health throughout the world.

Over the next couple of decades, South Asian governments carved out a role for the largest pool of unemployed people accessible to the state — unmarried women — to work in lower-income communities, villages and the neighbourhoods they belonged to.

## These women were publicly identified as 'volunteers', paving the way for a system that relied almost entirely on the caregiver ecosystem

These are the women we know as ASHA (Accredited Social Health Activist) workers in India. Close to 10 lakh ASHAs work across India.


![Image description](https://res.cloudinary.com/dl1gyw7jo/image/upload/v1777095718/blog/vikram/ouh4lr8q99buzw6vzegc.webp)

Most ASHA workers remain underpaid and the labour force at the centre of this organisation are women, many lower-caste — a demographic whose work remains historically undervalued. In this, they join the ranks of conservancy workers, domestic workers, agricultural workers and the thousands of other women in the informal and formal workforce, who are continuously kept away from a fair wage.

This is the story of the longest-ever indefinite day-and-night strike led by women in India's history. At its centre were hundreds of ASHA workers under KAHWA (Kerala ASHA Health Workers' Association), who pushed not just Kerala (where the strike was located), but the entire country to sit up and take notice.

## A walk with Rosamma

Our protagonist is a leader who emerged during this strike, like many others. Her name is Rosamma. One walk through her town with her, and like every other person who passes by and stops to talk, you might start calling her "Rosamma Chechi" too.

An eerie silence surrounded us as we waited at Yashoda's door, concern writ large on Rosamma Chechi's face. The house wore a dilapidated look, enveloped by the smell of moss and abandonment.

Rosamma Chechi walked into the house and instantly spotted Yashoda, seated at the far end, adjacent to the kitchen. It took Yashoda a minute or two to acknowledge them, but as soon as she recognised Rosamma Chechi, her eyes started to well up. Chechi, meanwhile, had walked into the kitchen, asking how long the pot of rice had been on the boil.

Yashoda seemed to ignore her, distracted, almost like it was her only escape from Chechi's questions. From the kitchen, Chechi made her way into Yashoda's bedroom, asking one question after the other, and finally emerged with a polythene bag. She fished out a diary and a paper bag of tablets, and gave a loud gasp.

"You've not taken any of these tablets!" she said to Yashoda.

## The structural contradiction

The ASHA scheme is a fundamental contradiction built into the architecture of Indian public health. The state depends on these workers for the last-mile delivery of virtually every health programme — maternal health, immunisation, tuberculosis control, malaria surveillance — while simultaneously refusing to classify them as workers deserving of a fixed wage, job security, or labour rights.

The incentive-based payment model, designed to keep costs low and maintain the fiction of 'voluntarism', has two consequences. First, it makes ASHA income precarious and unpredictable. Second, it provides a legal shield against the application of minimum wage legislation.

The Kerala strike broke that shield — not through courts, but through sustained collective action that made the contradiction impossible to ignore.

## What the strike achieved — and what it didn't

After 266 days, the government agreed to increase honoraria and implement some of the workers' demands. It was a partial victory. The fundamental question of employment status — whether ASHAs are workers or volunteers — remained unresolved.

That question matters enormously. Without formal employment status, ASHA workers remain outside the protections of labour law: no provident fund, no gratuity, no right to organise without risk of contract termination, no occupational safety standards for workers who routinely enter homes, walk remote roads at night, and handle infectious patients without adequate protective equipment.

The struggle in Kerala was not just about wages. It was about the terms on which women's work — particularly the care work that holds communities together — enters into the calculus of economic value.

## What primary healthcare tells us about everything else

The ASHA worker sits at the intersection of every structural inequality in Indian society: gender, caste, class, and the urban-rural divide. Her underpayment is not an oversight. It is a policy choice, sustained by the assumption that certain kinds of work — particularly work done by women, in homes and communities rather than offices and factories — can be extracted at below-market rates because the workers have no other option and the state has no political incentive to change.

The Kerala strike suggests that assumption can be challenged. It took 266 days, extraordinary organisational discipline, and the specific political ecology of a state with deep traditions of labour organising. Replicating it elsewhere will require different strategies.

But the argument has been made. The work is not voluntary. The workers are not volunteers. And the healthcare system that serves a billion and a half people cannot continue to rest on the unpaid and underpaid labour of the women who actually deliver it.
