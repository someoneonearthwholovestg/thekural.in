## Indian Railway

Indian Railways is one of the world's largest rail networks — a sprawling public enterprise that carries over 8 billion passengers annually and serves as the backbone of freight movement across the subcontinent. For decades it has been wholly government-owned, but the question of private participation has grown louder with each passing year.

## The case for private participation

The arguments in favour are well-rehearsed: private capital can fund capacity expansion without burdening the public exchequer, competition can drive service improvements, and professional management can unlock efficiencies that bureaucratic structures struggle to achieve.

India's experience with airport privatisation — particularly Delhi and Mumbai — is frequently cited as a template. Passenger experience improved markedly after private operators took charge, though critics note that profitability was secured partly through high user fees that excluded lower-income travellers.

## The complications

Railways are not airports. The social mandate of Indian Railways — subsidised passenger fares, connectivity to remote regions, employment of over 1.3 million people — makes a clean commercial logic impossible to apply.

Private operators entering the passenger segment would naturally gravitate toward high-yield routes: Mumbai–Delhi, Chennai–Bengaluru, Kolkata–Patna. The thin-margin rural corridors would remain with the public entity, which would then face the paradox of declining revenue on profitable routes while retaining all the loss-making obligations.

- Who absorbs the cost of cross-subsidisation?
- How are safety standards enforced across a mixed public-private network?
- What happens when a private operator exits a route mid-contract?

These are not hypothetical questions. They are the lived experience of rail privatisation in the United Kingdom, where the experiment has been partially reversed after decades of fragmentation, fare volatility, and persistent underinvestment in infrastructure.

## Where opportunity genuinely exists

The more productive conversation is about *what kind* of private participation, rather than whether privatisation should happen at all.

Freight corridors offer the clearest case. The Dedicated Freight Corridor (DFC) project, once complete, could be a genuine platform for private logistics operators — competing on service quality rather than owning track. The asset-light model, where the state owns infrastructure and private players pay access charges, has worked in aviation and could work here.

Station redevelopment is another area where private capital has a natural role. The monetisation of real estate around major stations — a global practice — can generate significant revenue without touching the core network operations.

Technology and rolling stock leasing are further avenues. Rather than manufacturing everything in-house, Railways can lease trains from private manufacturers on performance-linked contracts, shifting maintenance risk to the private sector while retaining operational control.

## What policy needs to get right

For any of this to work, the regulatory framework must be clarified before contracts are signed, not after. India currently lacks an independent rail regulator with genuine authority. The Railway Board acts simultaneously as owner, operator, and regulator — a structural conflict of interest that no amount of good intention can resolve.

An independent regulator would set access charges fairly, adjudicate disputes between public and private entities, enforce safety standards uniformly, and ensure that profitability on some routes genuinely cross-subsidises social obligations on others.

Without this, private participation will either fail to attract serious investors, or attract the wrong kind — those seeking regulatory capture rather than genuine value creation.

## Conclusion

The binary of "privatise" versus "keep public" is the wrong frame. Indian Railways needs capital, technology, and management expertise that the private sector can provide — but it also carries social obligations that pure market logic cannot honour.

The policy challenge is to design participation structures that harness private strengths while protecting public obligations. That requires patient institution-building, independent regulation, and a willingness to learn from both domestic experience and international precedent.

It is slow, unglamorous work. But it is the only kind that lasts.
